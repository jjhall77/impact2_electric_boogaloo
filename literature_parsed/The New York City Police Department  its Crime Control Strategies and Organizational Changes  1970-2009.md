## The New York City Police Department, its Crime Control Strategies and Organizational Changes, 1970-2009

#### Michael D. White

Over the last two decades, New York City has witnessed historic drops in crime. Numerous explanations for this crime decline have been discussed, and the New York City Police Department (NYPD) has been central to that debate, most notably because of the adoption of order maintenance policing and the implementation of Compstat. While those developments in the early 1990s are clearly important for understanding the potential role of the NYPD in the crime decline, those changes did not occur in a vacuum. This paper adopts an historical framework that places the role of the NYPD in the crime decline in the larger context of the department’s history, culture, and key events over a nearly 40-year span. This perspective suggests that many of the crime control strategies implemented by the NYPD over that time have been driven by internal and external crises, and that these strategies have also produced unintended consequences. With the historical analysis as a backdrop, the paper considers the ongoing debate over stop, question and frisk practices, and their disproportionate impact on minority residents, as the next potential crisis for the NYPD. The paper concludes with a discussion of the historical framework as a foundation for initiating a comparative dialog across law enforcement agencies regarding crime control strategies, their impact, and their consequences.

Numerous explanations for this crime decline have been offered, and the role of the New York City Police Department (NYPD) has been central to that contentious debate. Much of the focus on the NYPD has centered on the changes made to the Department in the early 1990s by then-Chief William Bratton, most notably the adoption of order maintenance policing and the implementation of Compstat. This paper adopts an historical framework to examine the NYPD and its crime control strategies over a period of nearly 40 years, highlighting the primary events——both in the city and the department——that have shaped the organization, its philosophy, structure, and operations over that time. First, the paper addresses basic questions about which strategies were used, why they were used (e.g. precipitating events), and whether there were any unintended consequences. Second, the paper considers whether the knowledge gained from this historical framework can shed light on our understanding of the NYPD’s current strategies, most notably with the department’s stop-question-frisk (SQF) tactic and its impact on police/minority community relations. Last, the paper explores whether this historical analysis can serve as a foundation for a comparative discussion of crime control strategies and their impact among law enforcement agencies across the USA. Overall, the primary thesis of the paper is that the historical framework enhances our understanding of the NYPD, its crime control strategies and their effects——both intended and unintended——and facilitates a more thoughtful discussion of the Department’s role in the New York City crime decline.

A Brief Note on Methodology and Organization

In order to identify the relevant literature on the NYPD, the author conducted searches on the primary criminal justice and criminology electronic databases (e.g. Criminal Justice Abstracts), employed “snowball sampling-style” identiﬁcation of documents (i.e. reviews of works cited in identiﬁed documents), and vetted the list of identiﬁed documents with several police scholars. Although the literature review was extensive, it would be ill-advised to claim that every existing report or article was uncovered. Nevertheless, the author is reasonably conﬁdent that the primary sources (through June 2012) have been reviewed in the current study.

This historical analysis demonstrates that the NYPD’s crime control strategies were often reformed in response to crises either within the department or in the city at large. As a result, the paper is organized chronologically based on the major internal and external events, and the department’s responses to those events. The ﬁrst four sections of the paper each cover roughly a decade. The discussions of the 1970s and 1980s are necessarily brief, but they set the stage for the more detailed examination of the strategies that characterized the NYPD during the 1990s and 2000s. The last section offers some ﬁnal thoughts on the implications of crisis-driven reform, the potential next emerging crisis for the department, and the need for comparative studies of crime control strategies across police departments.

The NYPD: 1970-1979 The First Crisis: the Knapp Investigation of the NYPD

During the early 1970s, the NYPD experienced a major corruption scandal that made frontpage news and led to the creation of the Knapp Commission (and later was made into the movie, Serpico). The Knapp Commission argued that the NYPD was largely organized around a value system that protected police corruption, and that the department maintained few if any effective strategies for preventing and detecting corrupt activities (Kane & White, forthcoming). The Knapp Commission’s (1972, p. 1) ﬁnal report indicates the pervasiveness of the problem:

We found corruption to be widespread. It took various forms depending upon the activity involved, appearing at its most sophisticated among plainclothesmen assigned to enforcing gambling laws. In the ﬁve plainclothes divisions where our investigations were concentrated we found a strikingly standardized pattern of corruption. Plainclothesmen, participating in what is known in police parlance as a pad, collected regular bi-weekly or monthly payments amounting to as much as $3,500 from each of the gambling establishments in the area under their jurisdiction, and divided the take in equal shares. The monthly share per man (called the nut) ranged from $300 and 400 in midtown Manhattan to $1,500 in Harlem. When supervisors were involved they received a share and a half. A newly assigned plainclothesman was not entitled to his share for about two months, while he was checked out for reliability, but the earnings lost by the delay were made up to him in the form of two months’ severance pay when he left the division.

The NYPD Post-Knapp: The First Wave of Reform

In the wake of this scandal, Mayor John Lindsay appointed reform Commissioner Patrick Murphy, who seized the opportunity to make sweeping changes to the NYPD. The Plainclothes Division, the gambling enforcement unit at the center of

the Serpico scandal, was abolished and the Organized Crime Control Bureau (OCCB) was created. Murphy placed strict control over ofﬁcers’ discretion through a general prohibition against gambling and prostitution enforcement unless there were speciﬁc complaints from outside the department (Skolnick & Fyfe, 1993). Murphy also created a ﬁeld associate program (whereby recent academy graduates served as undercover internal affairs investigators), developed an early warning system to identify troubled ofﬁcers, and required commanders to identify corruption hazards and create integrity plans for their precincts (Murphy & Plate, 1977). Internal affairs increased in both size and responsibility, and the disciplinary system was revamped.1 Murphy also introduced an “up or out” policy, which presumed that 20% of those at the rank of captain and above would be promoted or retire every year (Murphy & Plate, 1977). The NYPD also began hiring female police ofﬁcers in sizeable numbers for the ﬁrst time, implemented a revised training curriculum, set restrictive policies on deadly force and hot pursuits, and created a departmental career path.

Unintended Consequences of Reform

The emphasis on professionalism in the wake of Knapp dramatically altered the internal working environment of the department. Reuss-Ianni (1983) argued that its former monolithic cop culture had split into two. The ﬁrst, the street cop culture, adhered to the old values of loyalty to one’s peers and unit, and reﬂected the department’s pre-Knapp world view. The second, the management cop culture, was dedicated to professional and objective standards of performance, and was practiced by the upwardly mobile young supervisors and commanders (Reuss-Ianni, 1983). Reuss-Ianni (1983) outlined a series of rules that governed the street cop’s interaction with managers, such as “don’t give them too much activity,” “don’t do the bosses’ work for them,” and “don’t trust bosses to look out for your interest.” Reuss-Ianni (1983, p. 1) concluded that the implications of this split were profound, noting that “these two cultures are increasingly characterized by competing and often conﬂicting perspectives on procedure and practice in policing.” In simple terms, there was a large and growing disconnect between the decisions made in headquarters and the actions of ofﬁcers in the precincts.

Re-building and Re-orienting the NYPD: The Second Wave of Reform

As the ﬁnancial crisis passed the NYPD began an extensive hiring process to rebuild the force. From 1980 to 1984, the NYPD hired more than 12,000 ofﬁcers. This mass hiring overwhelmed the department’s Personnel Division, and as a result, many of the new ofﬁcers were hired without completed background investigations. In Perez’s (1994) terms, the NYPD’s efforts to quickly re-build its workforce to allow for “proper functioning” of the department may have come at the expense of quality control. In fact, many of the ofﬁcers who were hired during this time period never would have been appointed had their background investigations been completed. Kane and White (forthcoming) noted, for example, that 10% of the ofﬁcers hired by the NYPD from 1980 to 1984 had moderate to serious criminal histories.2

Notably, the NYPD partnered with the Vera Institute to assess how the 12,000 new ofﬁcers could best be used, leading to the department’s near-decade long experimentation with community-oriented policing (McElroy, Cosgrove, & Sadd, 1990). The program, called the community patrol ofﬁcer program (CPOP), was launched in July 1984 as a pilot in one precinct with 10 patrol ofﬁcers and one sergeant acting as planners, problem solvers, community organizers, and information exchange links (McElroy, Cosgrove, & Sadd, 1990). The NYPD expanded the program and by September 1988, each of the 75 precincts had a CPOP program. Mayor David Dinkins sought to institutionalize CPOP through the appointment of Lee Brown as Commissioner (a nationally recognized expert), and the passage of The Safe Streets, Safe City program which funded an additional 5,000 police ofﬁcers, as well as a variety of community prevention programs (Smith & Bratton, 2001). Brown made community policing the “dominant philosophy and strategy of the department” (Smith & Bratton, 2001, p. 468). An evaluation of CPOP showed that many of the program’s objectives were achieved as ofﬁcers became better acquainted with citizens, identiﬁed community problems and developed solutions to address them (Pate & Shtull, 1994).

Unintended Consequences of Reform

The NYPD continued to experience misconduct scandals throughout the 1980s and early 1990s, largely as a result of the mass hiring practices described above. In 1983, the NYPD was the subject of a congressional investigation on allegations of brutality, and in 1985, a small group of ofﬁcers was discovered used electronic stun guns to torture arrestees. Also in 1985, 13 ofﬁcers from Brooklyn’s 77th Precinct were criminally charged with stealing and trafﬁcking in drugs (the Buddy Boys). In 1993, a group of Brooklyn ofﬁcers was found to have engaged in brutality, robbery, theft, drug trafﬁcking, and abuse (leading to creation of the Mollen Commission). At the same time, a group of ofﬁcers in Harlem’s 30th Precinct——called “Nannery’s Raiders”——were discovered in the same sorts of activities, as were groups of ofﬁcers in Brooklyn’s 75th precinct and the 46th Precinct in the Bronx (Kane & White, forthcoming). The Mollen Commission (1994, p. 10) described the changing nature of misconduct, which was likely tied to the department’s earlier failure to screen out individuals with questionable backgrounds:

Today’s corruption is far more criminal, violent and premeditated than traditional notions of police corruption suggest and far more invidious than corruption of a generation ago. Testimony and ﬁeld investigations demonstrated that its most salient forms include groups of ofﬁcers protecting and assisting drug trafﬁckers for often sizeable proﬁts——stealing drugs, guns and moneyand often selling the stolen drugs and guns to or through criminal associates; committing burglary and robbery; conducting unlawful searches of apartments, cars and people; committing perjury and falsifying statements; and sometimes using excessive force, often in connection with corruption.

The NYPD: 1994-2000 The Third Crisis: Crime and Disorder in New York City

New York City, like many places across the USA, experienced dramatic increases in crime, violence, and disorder during the 1980s, much of it associated with the emergence of crack cocaine. For example, the number of homicides in New York jumped from 1,392 in 1985 to 2,262 in 1990 (a 60% increase), and remained above 2,000 through 1992. The level of social and physical disorder that emerged in conjunction with the violence was similarly destructive, both on the streets of New York and in the subway system.

By 1980, marijuana and heroin use was well-established and the use of cocaine and then crack cocaine became widespread … Drug sellers were active in every city park. Many blocks were converted to drug “supermarkets,” with as many as 20 or more drug sellers and their ever present customers … Passersby could easily observe persons injecting drugs, smoking crack or marijuana, and were routinely offered the opportunity to do the

same. Of particular concern in the mid-80s, crack selling crews became ever more reliant on guns and violence to enforce their business claims. (Johnson, Golub, & McCabe, 2010, p. 18)

Panhandling … was endemic. In addition to peaceful and passive beggars, stupeﬁed addicts slumped over cups … [and] panhandlers aggressively thrust their hands or cups into the faces of subway users and glowered at them … Often the recipients of donated clothing would strip and change clothes in the middle of stations or platforms, and with the lack of toilet facilities, many persons urinated or defecated in public places including train cars. At times, entire cars were taken over by indigents who sprawled over seats and on the ﬂoor. Approximately 1,200 to 2,000 persons a night were sleeping in the subway … Fare-beating created an additional sense of lawlessness. Estimated losses from the various fare scams and thefts ranged from $60 to 120 million a year, not to mention the indignation, demoralization and fear that paying passengers and on-site transit staff felt. Following on the heels of disorder and petty crimes, robbery and felonies started a steep increase in 1987. (Kelling & Coles, 1996, pp. 117-118)

The Emergence of Compstat and Order Maintenance Policing: The Third Wave of Reform

In the late 1980s, the leadership at the New York Transit Authority appointed William Bratton as Chief and, with George Kelling as an advisor, developed a broken windows-based enforcement strategy to address disorderly behavior and crime in the subway system. The initiative targeted aggressive panhandling, grafﬁti, fare-beating, and robberies——using both arrest and ejections from the subway system. In the months after Bratton took over, ejections from the subway and misdemeanor arrests both more than tripled. Within two years, felony crimes had dropped by more than 30% and a sense of order had been restored (Joanes, 2000). The turnaround in the subway system was dramatic, and in 1993, Mayor Rudy Giuliani named Bratton Commissioner of the NYPD. When Bratton took over the NYPD, there were a number of aspects of the department that he targeted for change. First, since the 1960s sociologists and criminologists had touted macro causes of crime (e.g. poverty), suggesting that police could do very little to control and prevent crime. In fact, as recently as 1994, Bayley (p. 3) wrote “The police do not prevent crime. This is one of the best kept secrets of modern life. Experts know it, the police know it, yet the police pretend that they are society’s best defense against crime.” Bratton sought to change this philosophy.

Second, this “root cause” philosophy was compounded by the overly passive nature of the NYPD crime control approach, which had emerged as a result of the department’s continued preoccupation with misconduct scandals. Third, Bratton (1998, pp. 208-209) believed that the department needed re-structuring, as Bureaus failed to effectively communicate with one another, and the department had little ability to examine crime statistics and trends in a timely

manner. Moreover, the precinct commander position——the important middle manager level——served as a “sunset post” for ofﬁcers on their way to retirement (Smith & Bratton, 2001). Johnson et al. (2010, p. 19) described the climate in the NYPD during the early 1990s:

Before Bratton, innovation and creativity were seen as threats to smooth operations and invitations for trouble. The NYPD had been organized as a strict hierarchy with approvals needed at all levels before any operational changes were allowed. This created a climate where the department was run by headquarters; ﬁeld commanders were rewarded not for their efforts in reducing crime and improving neighborhood conditions, but for keeping the status quo and avoiding problems.

Bratton’s Philosophical and Structural Changes

In terms of philosophy, Bratton attempted to eliminate the sense of resignation accompanying the “root-cause” explanation of crime (he publicly stated that the department would reduce crime by 10% in his ﬁrst year), and he embraced the broken windows thesis (discussed below). Bratton also adopted cutting-edge management theory from the private sector (Hammer & Champy, 1993) that highlighted management commitment, developing priorities, using creative approaches to accomplish tasks, and using information to guide decision-making (Willis et al., 2007). The new management philosophy resulted in numerous structural changes to the department, and culminated with the development of Compstat (deﬁned by timely and accurate information, rapid deployment of resources, effective tactics, follow-up, and assessment). Bratton’s Strategic and Operational Changes

The NYPD’s focus on crime and disorder was articulated in a series of strategic plans and several are worthy of attention here.5 First, given the success in the subway system, the NYPD targeted low-level social disorder and crime. As described in Reclaiming the Public Spaces of New York, the department began focusing its efforts on “grafﬁti, aggressive panhandling, fare beating, public drunkenness, unlicensed vending, public drinking, public urination and other misdemeanor offenses” (New York City Police Department [NYPD], 1994a). From 1993 to 1996 arrests rose by 23%, including a 40% increase in misdemeanor arrests and a 97% increase in drug arrests (Greene, 1999; see Harcourt (2001) for an alternative view).

The second noteworthy strategic initiative involved Getting Guns off the Streets of New York (NYPD, 1994b)——an intensive effort to curb gun violence through seizure of illegal ﬁrearms led by the Department’s Street Crime Unit. There were two important tactics employed by ofﬁcers to carry out this initiative. First, ofﬁcers engaged in “intensive scrutiny and follow-up of every incident, arrestee, or accomplice involving a gun, and follow-up of every lead on sources of guns” (Smith & Bratton, 2001, p. 480). Wintemute (2006) noted that in just three years, the NYPD made over 40,000 gun arrests and conﬁscated more than 50,000 guns.6 The second key feature of the department’s gun suppression efforts involved widespread use of Terry stops (Terry v. Ohio, 392 US 1, 1968), or stop-question-frisks (SQFs).

The third noteworthy strategic initiative involved drug enforcement. In Driving Drug Dealers out of New York (NYPD, 1994c, p. 15), the NYPD outlined its new approach which centered on driving “open-air drug activity off the streets of targeted areas, and then closing, and where possible seizing the inside drug trafﬁcking locations.” There were two elements to this new approach. First, in response to the limitations of prior initiatives (e.g. the tactical narcotics teams [TNT] in the 1980s),7 the department in 1995 created its Narcotics Initiative. The Initiative applied a problem-oriented policing approach to drug enforcement, with emphasis on identifying and ameliorating the underlying conditions

Unintended Consequences of Reform

There are two notable, unintended consequences of the reforms instituted during the 1990s. The ﬁrst involved the tremendous internal pressures that existed under the NYPD’s Compstat model. Eterno and Silverman (2005, 2010) have written two papers examining the negative consequences of the Compstat model, including its shift to greater reliance on aggressive practices and ofﬁcial sanctions, its increasing centralization of power, and its overemphasis on generating numbers rather than problem-solving, community buy-in and cooperation. They also note that while most patrol ofﬁcers are largely oblivious to the pressures of Compstat, the onus of the system has had a signiﬁcant negative impact on precinct commanders (Eterno & Silverman, 2005). For example, Thibault, Lynch, and McBride (2011, p. 130) stated:

During Bratton’s ﬁrst year in ofﬁce, two-thirds of the 76 precinct commanders were replaced. This process served to ﬂush the department of ill-performing and incompetent commanders. But some note that the process has continued, as the pressure to produce under the Compstat model has begun to drive out competent commanders. One retired middle-manager said, “First we got rid of commanders who were not competent but now, we are eating our own.”

Flynn (2000) described concerns among police union ofﬁcials that the pressures to produce were sometimes unreasonable, leading to low morale and a surge of retirements in the late 1990s. There is also some evidence to indicate that a handful of precinct commanders have succumbed to these pressures and falsiﬁed crime data. For example, a New York Times report in

- 2003 (Rashbaum, 2003) stated that ﬁve police commanders had been accused of altering data to present the appearance of crime declines (see also Eterno & Silverman, 2010).


Moreover, despite continued use of Compstat by the NYPD and many departments across the USA, questions have emerged regarding the extent to which it represents real innovation and reform. Weisburd, Willis, and Mastrofski have written a series of papers on Compstat (e.g. Weisburd, Mastrofski,

Willis, & Greenspan, 2005; Weisburd, McNally, Greenspan, & Willis, 2003), and their research highlights the gap between the promise of Compstat as innovation and the reality of its implementation. They argue that Compstat serves to reinforce the bureaucratic and paramilitary aspects of policing through increased command and control, and that it “stiﬂes rather than enhances creative problem-solving approaches, thereby allowing police to claim innovation without actually implementing any reforms” (Weisburd et al., 2005, p. 294). Other researchers have questioned the role of Compstat in the New York City crime decline, especially the drop in homicides (Eck & Maguire, 2000).

Compstat is yet to be proven as an effective crime control strategy in New York or in other cities that have adopted the Compstat approach. It is a sobering thought that Compstat has spread so widely across American police agencies absent strong empirical evidence that it actually does something about the crime problem. (Weisburd et al., 2005, p. 297)

The second unintended consequence of the reforms in the 1990s involves the continued and expanded use of the SQF strategy and the tension it has caused between the NYPD and minority residents. Greene (1999), for example, noted that from 1992 to 1996 legal ﬁlings of civil rights claims alleging abusive conduct increased by 75%, while the number of citizen complaints against police increased by 60%. Fagan, Geller, Davies, and West (2010, p. 310) noted that:

By the end of the decade, stops and frisks of persons suspected of crimes had become a ﬂashpoint for grievances by the City’s minority communities … In a ﬁfteen-month period from January 1998 through March 1999, non-Hispanic Black, Hispanic Black, and Hispanic White New Yorkers were three times more likely that their White counterparts to be stopped and frisked on suspicion of weapons or violent crimes.

Nevertheless, the number of stops has more than tripled in the 2000s, from 160,851 in 2003 to 575,996 in 2009 (Jones-Brown, Gill, & Trone, 2010). Though SQF has been used primarily as a tool in the NYPD efforts to seize guns, it has also emerged as a central component of the Department’s targeted effort against marijuana. Geller and Fagan (2010) reported that in 2006 alone there were 32,000 arrests for marijuana possession, a 500% increase from the previous decade.8

The NYPD’s heavy reliance on SQF has also resulted in two major lawsuits alleging racial proﬁling. The ﬁrst, Daniels et al. v. the City of New York, was ﬁled in 1999 by the Center for Constitutional Rights (CCR) and settled in 2003.

The NYPD: 2001-2009 The Fourth Crisis: September 11, 2001

At 8:46 am on September 11, 2001, American Airlines ﬂight 11 crashed into the north tower of the World Trade Center. Eighteen minutes later, United Airlines ﬂight 175 crashed into the south tower, and within 90 minutes both towers collapsed killing 2,749 people, including 343 New York City ﬁreﬁghters, 37 Port Authority ofﬁcers, and 23 NYPD ofﬁcers.

The attacks on the World Trade Center and the Pentagon on 11 September 2001, were among the most dramatic events ever to have occurred on US soil. They were unprecedented attacks by terrorists against icons of commercial and military power. (National Institute of Justice, 2009, p. 1)

The Infusion of Counterterrorism into the NYPD Mission: The Fourth Wave of Reform

The terrorist attacks on September 11, 2001 dramatically inﬂuenced the organizational philosophy, structure, and operations of the NYPD. Ray Kelly was appointed Commissioner of the NYPD by Mayor Michael Bloomberg in January 2002, and Kelly immediately set about infusing counterterrorism into the mission of the NYPD. The NYPD made a number of important structural and operational changes to support this mission. These include:

- • Creating a Counterterrorism Bureau——led by the Deputy Commissioner of Terrorism; staffed with 205 ofﬁcers; tasked with analyzing terrorist threats (and threat assessment), proactive intelligence gathering, running training exercises, conducting research, and developing detailed response plans for a variety of critical incidents.10
- • Shifting the mission of the Intelligence Division to focus almost exclusively on proactive terrorism prevention and detection, including the placement of intelligence ofﬁcers in each of the 76 precincts and the creation of a counterterrorism inspector position assigned to each patrol borough and major command.
- • Working intimately with the FBI’s Joint Terrorism Task Force, Department of Homeland Security, Coast Guard and other relevant local, state and federal agencies.
- • Providing counterterrorism training to all NYPD employees through recruit training at the academy, monthly roll call training, large-group mock exercises, and executive-level training (e.g. running command posts and resource mobilization).


10. Commissioner Kelly hired several high-level individuals with experience in counterterrorism, including Deputy Commissioner of Intelligence David Cohen, a former Operations Chief in the Central Intelligence Agency.

- • Re-focusing the Special Operations Division and its staff to protect potential terrorist targets in the city——this is accomplished through Operation Atlas which uses 1,000 ofﬁcers to patrol at sensitive locations and landmarks; the rotation of up to six helicopters in the air above the city; and Operation Hercules which seeks to disrupt terrorists’ surveillance by “providing the appearance of a randomized, heavily armed police presence.”
- • Providing up-to-date counterterrorism information to ofﬁcers through daily brieﬁng packages.
- • Developing a media relations plan to get accurate information out quickly to control rumors and misinformation.
- • Creating an anti-terrorist hotline with up to 120 detectives available to investigate tips.
- • Developing proactive efforts to recruit Arab-speaking ofﬁcers.
- • Enhancing training and response to bias-related crimes, particularly crimes against Muslim residents.
- • Creating partnerships with local and regional businesses to establish communication channels (called the Nexus Program; NIJ, 2009, pp. 5-9; see also Bornstein (2005).


Commissioner Kelly stated that “the integration of counter-terrorism into our mission ranks as one of the most important changes in our history” (NYPD, 2002, p. 1). More generally, the changes made to the NYPD reﬂect a widespread acceptance among scholars and practitioners that local law enforcement has a central role to play in terrorism prevention and response. For example, the Advisory Panel to Assess Domestic Response Capabilities for Terrorism (2001, p. 7) issued a report three months after the September 11th attacks, concluding that: “All terrorist incidents are local or at least will start that way. Effective response and recovery can only be achieved with the recognition that local responders are the ﬁrst line of defense.”11

The problems associated with the increased militarization of local police were discussed at length by Skolnick and Fyfe (1993). Skolnick and Fyfe (1993, p. 116) maintain that the “cops as soldiers” philosophy has served to deepen the “siege mentality” among police, and has put them in the position of ﬁghting a war they simply cannot win:

When any soldiers go to war, they must have enemies. When cops go to war against crime, their enemies are found in inner cities and among our minority populations. These emotions can affect ofﬁcers’ perceptions of and interactions with the community. The second consequence involves the Department’s relationship with the Arabic and Muslim communities in New York. Nislow (2001, p. 11) noted that the September 11th terrorist attacks fundamentally changed public perceptions of the police and racial proﬁling:

Bornstein (2005) highlighted the impact of the NYPD’s increased scrutiny on Arabic and Muslim residents, echoing Nislow’s (2001) point that many of the concerns associated with racial proﬁling of African-Americans are now increasingly evident in a new form of terrorist proﬁling. Bornstein (2005, p. 59) stated that:

Before 9/11, the debate was if there was or was not systematic racial proﬁling by police of black people. Everyone agreed that it was wrong, but they did not agree that it occurred. After 9/11, the debate became should there be or not be proﬁling of Arabs and Muslims.

In fact, in August 2011 the Associated Press published a report detailing an NYPD “human mapping” program that employs undercover ofﬁcers and surveillance techniques in Muslim communities (USA Today, 2011). The program, developed through a partnership with the CIA, has drawn signiﬁcant criticism

Given the central importance of cooperation from Arabic and Muslim residents to the success of the NYPD’s intelligence-gathering efforts, the potential alienation of these communities through such police practices represents a real and pressing concern.

Discussion Lessons Learned from the Historical Framework

The historical analysis of the NYPD and its crime control strategies over the last 40 years demonstrates that many of the reforms implemented by the department were driven by major events, both within the department itself and in the external environment. This ﬁnding is not unique to the NYPD, as police reforms have often come in the wake of crisis and scandal (Walker, 1977; Weisburd & Braga, 2006). In the case of the NYPD, this pattern of crisis-driven reform can be traced back to the 1970s with Commissioner Murphy’s changes in the wake of the Serpico scandal. Similarly, following the citywide ﬁscal crisis in the late 1970s, the NYPD hired 12,000 police ofﬁcers and began a near-decade long experiment with community policing. The explosion of crime and violence during the 1980s and early 1990s led to the re-shaping of the NYPD through order maintenance policing and the development of Compstat. And the September 11th terrorist attacks led to the infusion of counterterrorism into the mission of the NYPD.

However, the historical analysis also demonstrated that, in each decade, the reforms instituted by the NYPD led to unintended consequences. For example, Murphy’s changes to the department led to a schism between line ofﬁcers and managers that persisted throughout the 1980s and has seemed to intensify under Compstat (Eterno & Silverman, 2005). The mass hiring in the early 1980s led to series of misconduct scandals over the next decade, culminating with the Mollen Commission. The adoption of the SQF tactic in the 1990s, and its expanded use more recently, has strained the department’s relationship with minority residents. And the infusion of counterterrorism into the police mission has raised questions about increased militarism, and the treatment of Arabic and Muslim citizens. Of course, many of these events transpired at the same time that New York City has witnessed a long-term, historic drop in crime that has continued into the twenty-ﬁrst century.

The results from this historical analysis lead to questions about the next major crisis that will confront the NYPD, as well as how the department will respond to it. It is impossible to do any more than speculate at this point, but there are clear indications that strained relations between the NYPD and minority residents, as a result of expanded use of the SQF strategy, offer fertile ground for the next major crisis. In 2011, 685,724 residents were stopped by police on the street (a 600% increase in less than a decade) and nearly 9 in 10 of those stops involve a nonwhite citizen (Baker, 2012). During the ﬁrst three

months of 2012, the number of stops was on pace to exceed 730,000 by the end of the year, with an average of more than 2,200 stops per day (Baker, 2012). The debate over SQF has become a contentious issue in the 2012 New York City mayoral campaign, and the New York City Council recently proposed the creation of an independent monitor for the NYPD (Chen, 2012). Moreover, on 16 May 2012, the US District Court granted class action status to the plaintiffs in Floyd et al. v. New York et al. (http://ccrjustice.org/ﬁles/5-16-12%20Floyd% 20Class%20Cert%20Opinion%20and%20Order.pdf). If the tensions caused by the SQF strategy persist and emerge as the next major crisis facing the NYPD, it is unclear how the department will respond. The NYPD leadership has been steadfast in their belief that SQF is an effective crime control tactic that does not involve racial proﬁling. Commissioner Kelly recently said, “I would submit that our strategies are saving lives” (Baker, 2012).

Even if the strategy is contributing to the low levels of crime, the unintended consequences may be signiﬁcant. Tyler (2006) noted that individuals’ perceptions of the police are greatly affected by how they are treated. If people are treated fairly——e.g. they receive “procedural justice”——they are more likely to view the police as a legitimate authority. Tyler (2006) goes on to state that individuals who see the police as legitimate are more likely to cooperate, are more likely to comply with police requests, and are more likely to follow the law. Alternatively, citizens who believe they are treated unfairly are less likely to see the police as legitimate, perhaps leading to lower levels of cooperation, deference, and compliance with the law. These connections between procedural justice, legitimacy, cooperation, and compliance provide an important backdrop for examining developments in New York. Quite simply, continued widespread use of SQF by the NYPD against predominately minority citizens, coupled with the pervasive belief among minority residents that the stops are unjustiﬁed, may not only have long-lasting negative effects on police-community relations, it may lead to less effective crime control in the future as vital lines of communication, intelligence, and cooperation are severed.

The Historical Framework as a Starting Point for Comparative Study

The primary thesis of this paper is that the current philosophy, structure, and strategies of the NYPD have been shaped by events unique to New York City and the department over the last 40 years, and any discussion of the NYPD’s role in the New York crime decline must consider this history. Though this thesis presumes that comparisons made between the NYPD and other police departments will be necessarily limited given each agency’s unique history and culture (e.g. comparing apple and oranges), it is important to note that there are shared experiences as well. For example, the struggles with police misconduct and ﬁscal problems in the 1970s, the emergence of crack cocaine and associated crime in the 1980s, and the infusion of counterterrorism into the

police mission in the 2000s were not unique to New York (e.g. police departments may be apples and oranges but they are all still fruit).12 Also, other police departments have had their own experiences with elements of the NYPD crime control approach. For instance, Greene (1999, p. 185) compared the NYPD and the San Diego Police Department, noting that both cities experienced similar crime declines in the 1990s despite a very different communityoriented approach in San Diego that Greene describes as “more efﬁcient and humane.” The Los Angeles Police Department (LAPD) began using order maintenance policing (called the Safer Cities Initiative) in the skid row section of Los Angeles in 2006, and since then, crime has dropped signiﬁcantly (including a 46% drop in Part I crime; LAPD 2011). But the Initiative has raised concerns among community advocates and scholars regarding treatment of the homeless by police (see Blasi, 2007; Blasi & Stuart, 2008; The Los Angeles Community Action Network, 2010; White, 2010). The Philadelphia Police Department (PPD) has embraced the SQF strategy, stopping more than 250,000 citizens in 2009. Notably, violent crime has dropped in Philadelphia since adoption of the SQF strategy, but in November 2010 the ACLU of Pennsylvania ﬁled a lawsuit in Federal Court alleging that the PPD was engaged in widespread racial proﬁling.

These developments raise questions about whether the experiences of other police departments hold any value for the NYPD, and vice versa. And perhaps more importantly, do these shared experiences help to inform discussions of how to improve policing in the future? For instance, if heavy reliance on police-initiated stops of citizens seems to be effective in reducing crime but the practice also produces harmful consequences, and these consequences emerge across several jurisdictions, perhaps police leadership organizations should more closely examine the practice and consider its crime-reduction beneﬁts in the context of its damaging, unintended side effects. Also, there are emerging questions surrounding the negative consequences of Compstat in New York, including its impact on middle managers and whether it facilitates a return to the means-over-ends syndrome that Goldstein (1979) warned about (i.e. where the generation of numbers becomes the outcome of interest rather than solving problems). Have these issues also emerged in other departments that have implemented Compstat, and if so, how have those departments responded? Similar parallels can be drawn among police departments regarding the infusion of counterterrorism in the local police mission.

In sum, this paper posits that one cannot fully understand the NYPD of the twenty-ﬁrst century and its role in the New York City crime decline without placing the Department in a larger historical context. While this historical framework highlights the uniqueness of the NYPD, it also underscores the fact that much of this history is shared rather than exclusive. As a result, the experiences of other police departments across the country offer important insights for the NYPD to draw upon (and vice versa), particularly with regard to order maintenance policing, Compstat, counterterrorist policing, and perhaps most
