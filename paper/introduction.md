# Introduction

Place-based policing has become one of the most empirically supported strategies in modern law enforcement. A large body of evidence—synthesized across multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses—demonstrates that concentrating police resources at small geographic "hot spots" produces statistically significant crime reductions without simply displacing offending to nearby areas (Braga et al. 2019; Weisburd & Telep 2014). Yet a critical question remains largely unanswered: what happens to crime when intensive enforcement at these hot spots is suddenly withdrawn?

The New York City Police Department's Operation Impact (2003–2012) offers a rare opportunity to study both sides of this question. The program deployed newly graduated officers to designated high-crime "impact zones" across the city, combining visible police presence with aggressive investigative stops under the department's stop, question, and frisk (SQF) authority. At its peak, NYPD officers conducted nearly 700,000 stops per year, a substantial share of which occurred within impact zones. MacDonald, Fagan, and Geller (2016) estimated the program's effects using a difference-in-differences design with conditional fixed effects Poisson regression, finding that impact zones experienced a 12% reduction in total reported crime, with particularly large decreases in burglary and robbery. Neighboring areas also saw crime declines—evidence of diffusion rather than displacement. Critically, however, the authors' dose-response specification revealed that only stops supported by probable cause were associated with crime reductions; the far more numerous stops based on general suspicion had no measurable effect on crime.

Two institutional shocks shortly after the study period created what amounts to a natural experiment in the rapid withdrawal of proactive enforcement. In August 2013, Judge Shira Scheindlin ruled in *Floyd v. City of New York* that the NYPD's SQF practices violated the Fourth and Fourteenth Amendments, finding a pattern of unconstitutional stops that disproportionately targeted Black and Hispanic New Yorkers. The ruling, combined with the January 2014 inauguration of Mayor Bill de Blasio—who had campaigned on police reform—precipitated a dramatic collapse in stop activity. Annual SQF encounters fell from approximately 685,000 in 2011 to fewer than 46,000 by 2015, a decline of over 93%. Operation Impact itself was formally restructured, with the de Blasio administration replacing the program's enforcement-heavy posture with a community policing model.

This paper makes two contributions. First, we conduct a de novo replication of MacDonald, Fagan, and Geller (2016) using the original data and estimation strategy, confirming that all twenty Model 1 coefficients fall within 3% of the published values and that the core findings are robust across specifications. We extend the replication with modern heterogeneity-robust difference-in-differences estimators—including the Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) and Sun and Abraham (2021) approaches—demonstrating that the original two-way fixed effects estimates are not contaminated by forbidden comparisons across treatment cohorts. A Bacon (2021) decomposition confirms that 65% of the identifying variation comes from clean treated-versus-untreated comparisons.

Second, and more substantively, we extend the analysis through 2019 to examine the consequences of the post-2012 enforcement collapse. By appending incident-level crime, arrest, and SQF data from NYC Open Data to the original panel, we construct a dataset spanning both the high-enforcement period (2004–2012) and the post-enforcement period (2013–2019), ending before COVID-19 disrupts the crime data. This extended panel allows us to directly test competing theoretical predictions about what should happen when intensive hot spots enforcement is removed. Standard deterrence theory (Nagin, Solow & Lum 2015) predicts that crime should rebound as offenders' perceived risk of apprehension declines. Community disruption accounts (Sullivan & O'Keeffe 2017) predict the opposite—that the withdrawal of aggressive enforcement should reduce crime by eliminating the destabilizing effects of mass stops on community social organization. A third possibility, supported by evidence that marginal enforcement reductions do not increase crime (Cho, Goncalves & Weisburst 2021), is that the SQF decline had no detectable effect on crime because the stops that disappeared were predominantly the ones MacDonald et al. found to be ineffective.

We exploit the post-Floyd enforcement collapse using three complementary identification strategies. First, we augment the original conditional fixed effects Poisson specification with indicators distinguishing the active-enforcement and post-enforcement periods, testing whether the treatment effect of impact zone designation persists, attenuates, or reverses after enforcement intensity drops. Second, we estimate an event study around the Floyd ruling with a ±24-month window, tracing the dynamic path of crime in impact zones relative to controls before and after the institutional shock. Third, we implement a difference-in-difference-in-differences design that interacts impact zone status, treatment activation timing, and enforcement era, directly estimating whether the interaction of zone designation and enforcement intensity drives crime outcomes.

The paper also examines racial disparities in enforcement within impact zones. MacDonald and Fagan (2019) showed that the July 2007 expansion of impact zones increased stops disproportionately for Black and Hispanic residents, even though weapon recovery rates did not differ by race. We extend this analysis across the full study period, testing whether the racial composition of enforcement changed as overall stop volume collapsed and whether any crime effects varied across neighborhoods with different racial compositions.

Several features of the NYC context make this setting particularly informative for the broader literature. Unlike de-policing episodes driven by viral incidents of police violence—which Devi and Fryer (2020) show can trigger large crime increases through officer demoralization—the post-Floyd enforcement decline was driven by a judicial ruling without an accompanying viral deadly force event. This distinction matters because it isolates the effect of enforcement withdrawal from the confounding effects of police-community crisis. Additionally, the post-2012 period coincided with continued economic growth, gentrification, and demographic change in many former impact zone neighborhoods, allowing us to examine whether structural improvements to the built environment sustained crime reductions independently of police enforcement.

Understanding whether the crime reductions achieved by Operation Impact survived the withdrawal of enforcement has direct implications for the design of place-based policing strategies. If crime gains persist after enforcement declines, it suggests that intensive policing may catalyze durable changes in neighborhood conditions—target hardening, community development, or sustained incapacitation of high-risk offenders—that outlast the police presence itself. If instead crime rebounds, it confirms the Sorg et al. (2013) finding from the Philadelphia Foot Patrol Experiment that hot spots policing produces no residual deterrence, implying that sustained deployment is necessary to maintain crime reductions. And if crime remains stable despite the enforcement collapse, it raises the possibility that the original crime reductions were driven primarily by police presence—not investigative stops—and that the massive expansion of SQF within impact zones imposed substantial costs on communities (Tyler, Fagan & Geller 2014; Petersen et al. 2023) without proportional public safety benefits.
