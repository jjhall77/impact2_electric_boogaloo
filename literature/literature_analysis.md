## Phase 1: Mapping the Lineage (The Rise and Fall of Proactive Policing)

### Question 1: Historical Evolution of Theories Linking Police Surges and SQF to Crime Concentration

The literature traces a clear arc from theoretical foundations through empirical validation to critical reassessment.

**Stage 1 — Deterrence Through Presence (1990s–2000s).** The earliest theoretical foundation comes from Koper (1995), who used survival analysis on Minneapolis Hot Spots Patrol data to demonstrate that police presence at hot spots produces diminishing returns, with an optimal dosage of approximately 10–15 minutes per visit. This "Koper curve" established that brief, rotating police visits could generate deterrent residuals lasting roughly 15 minutes after departure. The implication was that police *presence* — not necessarily enforcement activity — was the active ingredient in crime suppression.

Nagin, Solow & Lum (2015) formalized this intuition mathematically, developing a model showing that police visibility in hot spots alters the distribution of criminal opportunities by heightening offenders' perceived risk of apprehension. Their model demonstrates why concentrated deployment is more efficient than random patrol and, crucially, argues that police affect crime primarily through shaping criminal opportunities rather than through post-crime apprehension. This framework predicts that presence, not arrest volume, drives deterrence.

**Stage 2 — Experimental Validation (2009–2013).** The Philadelphia Foot Patrol Experiment (Ratcliffe et al. 2011) provided the strongest experimental evidence: 249 additional officers deployed to 60 treatment hot spots reduced violent crime by 23% relative to controls during the 12-week experiment. No significant displacement was observed. This result was directly analogous to Operation Impact's deployment logic.

However, the follow-up study by Sorg et al. (2013) revealed a critical limitation: deterrent effects showed *no residual persistence* after foot patrols were withdrawn. Beats staffed for 22 weeks even showed decaying effects during the experiment. This finding — that hot spots policing works only while police are actively deployed — has profound implications for sustained programs like Operation Impact (2003–2012).

**Stage 3 — The SQF Question (2014–2019).** As the evidence base matured, attention shifted from *whether* police presence reduces crime to *what police do* at hot spots. Weisburd et al. (2015) found that SQF produces a "significant yet modest" approximately 2% deterrent effect on crime at the micro-geographic level, but acknowledged an inability to separate stop effects from deployment effects. The National Academies (2018) review concluded that SQF as a general citywide strategy has mixed evidence, while focused uses targeting gun crime at specific places show more consistent effects.

MacDonald, Fagan & Geller (2016) sharpened this distinction by demonstrating that within Operation Impact zones, only probable-cause stops were associated with crime reductions, while general suspicion stops had no measurable crime reduction effect. This decomposition — the same police surge, but different mechanisms at work — marked a turning point.

**Stage 4 — Critical Reassessment (2019–2025).** The most recent literature distinguishes between hot spots strategies themselves. Tregle, Tillyer & Smith (2025) found that high-visibility deterrence strategies had *no effect* on self-initiated officer activity while still reducing crime, whereas proactive offender-focused approaches increased enforcement burden without clearly superior crime effects. Petersen et al. (2023) synthesized the full evidence base, finding that police stops produce a 13% area-level crime reduction but simultaneously increase mental health issues (46%), physical health issues (36%), and negative attitudes toward police — leading them to conclude that alternative policing interventions may achieve similar results with fewer costs.

**The evolution in summary:** The field moved from "police presence deters crime" (Koper 1995) → "experimental confirmation at hot spots" (Ratcliffe 2011) → "but SQF specifically may not be the active ingredient" (MacDonald et al. 2016; Weisburd et al. 2015) → "and the costs of SQF may outweigh its marginal benefits" (Petersen et al. 2023; Tyler et al. 2014).

### Question 2: Foundational Authors on Withdrawal of Proactive Policing

**Core authors and their theoretical frameworks:**

**Sullivan & O'Keeffe (2017)** provide the most provocative framing. Writing in *Nature Human Behaviour*, they analyze the NYPD "slowdown" of late 2014/early 2015 — a sharp reduction in proactive policing following the killing of two NYPD officers — and argue that the curtailment of proactive policing actually *reduced* major crime. Their theoretical frame draws on labeling theory and community-disruption arguments: aggressive enforcement of minor offenses destabilizes communities and generates conditions conducive to more serious crime. Under this theory, the massive drop in SQF around 2013–2014 should *improve* public safety.

**Chalfin, Mitre-Becerril & Williams (2024)** directly challenge Sullivan & O'Keeffe in a formal replication study. After correcting for data errors, alternative specifications, and timing issues, they find that the original result "disappears or reverses." Their conclusion is that there is no robust evidence that curtailing proactive policing in NYC reduced major crime. This replication failure is critical because it eliminates the strongest empirical claim that de-policing is beneficial.

**Devi & Fryer (2020)** offer the most nuanced framework. Analyzing 42 DOJ pattern-or-practice investigations, they find a crucial moderator: investigations preceded by viral incidents of deadly force led to approximately 893 excess homicides and 34,000 excess felonies, while investigations *without* viral incidents actually reduced crime. The mechanism is abrupt, demoralization-driven de-policing — e.g., Chicago police-civilian interactions dropped approximately 90% in the month after investigation announcement. Their framework predicts that the *context* of enforcement withdrawal matters: a court-ordered reform (Floyd v. NYC) without a viral deadly force incident should produce a different trajectory than the post-Ferguson pattern.

**Cho, Goncalves & Weisburst (2021)** exploit line-of-duty officer deaths as exogenous shocks that reduce arrest activity by 5–10%. Despite these drops in enforcement — including a 20% reduction in traffic stops — they find *no evidence of crime increase*. Their framework suggests that marginal enforcement activity (precisely the kind of discretionary SQF that Operation Impact generated) can be reduced without public safety costs.

**Sorg et al. (2013)** provide the experimental evidence for what happens when police withdraw from hot spots. After the Philadelphia Foot Patrol Experiment ended, deterrent effects evaporated. No residual deterrence was observed. Displacement that appeared during the experiment decayed within 3 months. This predicts that the withdrawal of Operation Impact should have reversed crime gains — unless something else replaced the deterrent function.

**What the theories predict for the 2013–2014 SQF collapse:**

| Theory | Prediction |
|--------|-----------|
| **Deterrence (Nagin et al. 2015)** | Crime should increase as perceived risk of apprehension drops |
| **Community disruption (Sullivan & O'Keeffe 2017)** | Crime should decrease as community destabilization from aggressive enforcement ends |
| **De-policing via demoralization (Devi & Fryer 2020)** | Depends on context; Floyd (no viral incident) predicts modest or no crime increase |
| **Marginal enforcement (Cho et al. 2021)** | No crime increase from reducing discretionary enforcement |
| **Residual deterrence (Sorg et al. 2013)** | Crime should rebound as deterrent effect of presence decays post-withdrawal |
| **Place-based stability (Weisburd & Telep 2014)** | Hot spots persist regardless of enforcement; crime concentrations are structurally determined |

The conflicting predictions make the post-2012 extension an unusually powerful test: the same city, the same measurement framework, but a natural experiment in rapid enforcement withdrawal.

## Phase 2: Uncovering the Friction (The De-Policing Debate)

### Question 3: Primary Areas of Disagreement on Consequences of Reducing SQF

The literature reveals three distinct fault lines:

**Fault Line 1: Does reducing stops increase crime?**

The sharpest disagreement is between Sullivan & O'Keeffe (2017) and Chalfin et al. (2024). Sullivan & O'Keeffe claim that curtailing proactive policing *reduced* major crime during the NYPD slowdown, interpreting this as evidence that aggressive enforcement incites crime. Chalfin et al.'s replication demolishes this finding, showing it was driven by specific modeling choices and data construction decisions. After corrections, the result disappears.

Meanwhile, Devi & Fryer (2020) find that the answer is *conditional*: enforcement withdrawal preceded by viral incidents increases crime dramatically, but court-ordered reforms without viral incidents do not. Cho et al. (2021) find that marginal reductions in arrest activity produce no detectable crime increase across 2,000+ municipalities.

MacDonald et al. (2016) themselves provide a partial resolution: it depends on *which* stops are reduced. Their Model 4 (dose-response) shows that only probable-cause stops reduce crime. General suspicion stops — which constituted the bulk of SQF and experienced the largest post-Floyd decline — had no crime reduction effect. This predicts that the post-2013 SQF collapse should have minimal crime impact, since the stops that disappeared were predominantly the ineffective kind.

**Fault Line 2: Presence vs. enforcement as the active ingredient.**

Nagin et al. (2015) argue theoretically that police affect crime through opportunity-shaping (visibility), not apprehension. This aligns with MacDonald et al.'s findings but conflicts with the traditional deterrence view that the *threat* of arrest (operationalized through SQF) is what deters.

Weisburd et al. (2015) cannot separate these mechanisms empirically, finding SQF effects but acknowledging confounding with deployment. Tregle et al. (2025b) provide the cleanest test: in Dallas, high-visibility deployment with *no increase* in officer-initiated activity still reduced crime, while offender-focused approaches increased enforcement burden. This supports the presence hypothesis.

The National Academies (2018) report attempts synthesis, concluding that place-based strategies generally reduce crime but that the specific role of investigative stops (as distinct from officer presence) remains "difficult to disentangle."

**Fault Line 3: Whether the *costs* of SQF outweigh its crime benefits.**

Even authors who find positive crime effects of SQF disagree on the net assessment. Petersen et al. (2023) find a 13% area-level crime reduction from stop interventions — remarkably consistent with MacDonald et al.'s 12% — but document severe individual-level harms: 46% increase in mental health issues, 36% increase in physical health issues, and negative attitudinal effects. They conclude that alternative policing interventions should be preferred.

Tyler, Fagan & Geller (2014) document that SQF undermines police legitimacy among young men, reducing willingness to cooperate with law enforcement — potentially generating a long-run crime *increase* through eroded social control. Goel et al. (2016) show that the vast majority of stops (over 40% of weapon stops had <1% hit rates) produced no law enforcement value while imposing costs on communities.

In contrast, Chalfin & McCrary (2018) argue from a welfare economics perspective that U.S. cities are substantially underpoliced, with each additional dollar spent on police yielding $1.63 in reduced victim costs. Under their framework, the crime reduction benefits of programs like Operation Impact may justify their costs.

### Question 4: Stability of Micro-Places When Police Presence Changes

The literature is surprisingly convergent on micro-place stability, even as it disagrees on enforcement effects.

**Hot spots are structurally determined, not enforcement-determined.** Weisburd & Telep (2014) identify this as a foundational principle of the hot spots literature: crime concentrates at specific micro-places due to environmental and social features (routine activities, place management, physical design), not solely due to police presence or absence. When police withdraw, the *concentration* of crime at hot spots persists because the underlying generators remain.

**But intensity fluctuates with policing.** Sorg et al. (2013) show that crime *levels* at hot spots increase when police withdraw — the Philadelphia experiment showed no residual deterrence after patrols ended — even though the *relative concentration* of crime at those places persists. This is a crucial distinction: the hot spot remains a hot spot, but the temperature rises.

Koper (1995) quantifies the temporal dynamics: after police leave a hot spot, the deterrent residual lasts approximately 15 minutes before decay begins. For sustained programs like Operation Impact (years of deployment), the withdrawal prediction depends on whether any structural change occurred during the treatment period — e.g., target hardening, community development, offender incapacitation — that could sustain reductions independently of police presence.

**Displacement is rare; diffusion is common.** Braga et al.'s (2019) meta-analysis of 65 studies finds that diffusion of crime prevention benefits into adjacent areas is more common than displacement. MacDonald et al. (2016) confirm this for Operation Impact: Model 2 shows neighboring census block groups experienced approximately 7% crime reductions. This suggests that when police *leave* an impact zone, we should expect crime to increase at the hot spot itself but not necessarily a "hydraulic" spread to surrounding areas.

**Citywide effects may persist.** Tregle et al. (2025a) find that hot spots policing can produce citywide crime reductions extending beyond designated hot spots. If this is correct, then the withdrawal of Operation Impact could have consequences beyond the former impact zones — or alternatively, citywide crime trends (which continued declining after 2012) may reflect factors other than hot spots enforcement.

**The synthesis for the extension analysis:** Crime hot spots should remain geographically stable after the withdrawal of Operation Impact (the same blocks remain high-crime), but the *level* of crime at those spots should increase if deterrence was the primary mechanism. The key empirical question is whether the post-2012 increase (if any) is proportional to the loss of police presence, or whether the structural crime decline in NYC was driven by other factors (demographics, incarceration, economic conditions) that continued operating independently.

## Phase 3: The NYC Methodology Deep-Dive (Measuring a Structural Break)

### Question 5: Analytical Techniques for Measuring Policy Interventions and Structural Breaks

The papers employ a diverse toolkit. Here is a comprehensive inventory:

**1. Conditional Fixed Effects Poisson Regression (FE Poisson / fepois)**
*Used by:* MacDonald et al. (2016)
*Definition:* Count data model with unit-specific fixed effects that conditions out time-invariant unobservables. Appropriate for non-negative integer outcomes (crime counts) with overdispersion. Estimates within-unit changes in crime associated with treatment onset. Fixed effects at the precinct × year-month level absorb time-varying shocks common to all units within a precinct-month.
*Strengths:* Handles count data naturally; fixed effects control for unobserved heterogeneity; robust to distributional misspecification.

**2. Difference-in-Differences (DiD)**
*Used by:* MacDonald et al. (2016), Cho et al. (2021), Devi & Fryer (2020), MacDonald & Fagan (2019)
*Definition:* Compares changes in outcomes over time between treatment and control groups, netting out common time trends and time-invariant group differences. Identifies causal effects under the parallel trends assumption.
*Variants in corpus:*
- Standard two-group two-period DiD (MacDonald et al. 2016, Model 1)
- Staggered DiD with multiple treatment cohorts and timing (as in the project's Phase 3 extensions)
- Triple-difference / DDD (MacDonald & Fagan 2019): adds a third differencing dimension (race) to the spatial and temporal variation

**3. Difference-in-Difference-in-Differences (DDD)**
*Used by:* MacDonald & Fagan (2019)
*Definition:* Extends DiD by adding a third dimension of variation — in this case, race. Compares changes in stops across time, across space (impact zones vs. non-impact zones), and across racial groups. Uses entropy distance weighting for a doubly robust estimator.

**4. Interrupted Time Series Analysis (ITSA)**
*Used by:* Sullivan & O'Keeffe (2017), Tregle et al. (2025a)
*Definition:* Models outcome trends before and after a discrete intervention point, estimating both level changes (intercept shift) and slope changes (trend shift). Requires a clear, known intervention date and sufficient pre/post observations.
*Strengths:* Does not require a control group (though can incorporate one). Natural for abrupt policy changes.
*Limitation:* Vulnerable to confounding events coinciding with the intervention.

**5. Synthetic Control Method (SCM)**
*Used by:* Devi & Fryer (2020)
*Definition:* Constructs a weighted combination of untreated units that matches the pre-treatment trajectory of the treated unit. The post-treatment divergence between the treated unit and its synthetic control estimates the causal effect. Particularly suited for single-unit studies (one city, one department).
*Strengths:* Transparent; provides a visual counterfactual; handles heterogeneous treatment effects naturally.

**6. Regression Discontinuity (RD)**
*Used by:* Sullivan & O'Keeffe (2017, implicitly)
*Definition:* Exploits a sharp cutoff in an assignment variable to estimate causal effects. In the policing context, the "running variable" is time, and the discontinuity is a discrete policy shock (e.g., the NYPD slowdown date).
*Note:* Sullivan & O'Keeffe use this in a temporal regression discontinuity framework, which Chalfin et al. (2024) critique as sensitive to bandwidth and specification.

**7. Event Study Designs**
*Used by:* Cho et al. (2021), Devi & Fryer (2020), MacDonald et al. (2016, Model 3)
*Definition:* Estimates dynamic treatment effects across multiple pre- and post-treatment periods. Plots coefficient paths to test for pre-trends (validating parallel trends) and trace out the timing of treatment effects.
*MacDonald et al. use:* Model 3 with lead/lag indicators (eventneg2, eventneg1, eventpos1, eventpos2) around impact zone activation.

**8. Survival Analysis**
*Used by:* Koper (1995), Sorg et al. (2017)
*Definition:* Models time-to-event data — specifically, the time until crime/disorder resumes after police departure (Koper) or the time officers remain in their assigned beats (Sorg). The "Koper curve" is a survival function for deterrence decay.

**9. Multilevel / Hierarchical Models**
*Used by:* Gelman et al. (2007), Goel et al. (2016), Sorg et al. (2013)
*Definition:* Accounts for nested data structures (stops within precincts, crimes within neighborhoods). Allows estimation of both within-group and between-group variation. Gelman et al. use multilevel Poisson regression; Sorg et al. use multilevel growth curves.

**10. Space-Time Interaction Models**
*Used by:* Weisburd et al. (2015)
*Definition:* Panel models that exploit variation in both spatial units (street segments) and temporal units (days/weeks) to estimate treatment effects. Requires geocoded, high-frequency data.

**11. Bayesian Hierarchical Models**
*Used by:* Goel et al. (2016)
*Definition:* Estimates ex ante probabilities (e.g., probability of weapon recovery for each stop) using a Bayesian framework that partially pools information across precincts and racial groups.

**12. Meta-Analysis (Random Effects)**
*Used by:* Braga et al. (2019), Petersen et al. (2023)
*Definition:* Statistically combines effect sizes across multiple studies to estimate an overall treatment effect. Random effects models account for between-study heterogeneity.

**13. Structural Equation Modeling (SEM)**
*Used by:* Tyler et al. (2014)
*Definition:* Models causal pathways between observed variables through latent constructs. Tyler et al. model the pathway: stops → procedural justice perceptions → legitimacy → law-abiding behavior.

**14. Discriminant Analysis**
*Used by:* Gelman et al. (2007, referenced in racial bias analyses)
*Definition:* Identifies variables that best discriminate between outcome categories (e.g., arrest vs. no arrest, weapon found vs. not found).

**15. Permutation Tests**
*Used by:* MacDonald et al. (2016)
*Definition:* Nonparametric inference by randomly reshuffling treatment assignment 1,000 times and comparing the true estimate to the placebo distribution. Tests whether the observed effect could arise by chance under the null hypothesis of no treatment effect.

### Question 6: Most Robust Frameworks for Isolating the Effect of Sudden Enforcement Withdrawal

For the specific task of extending the Operation Impact analysis through 2016 to capture the 2013–2014 SQF plummet, the papers identify several frameworks as particularly robust:

**1. Difference-in-Differences with Staggered Treatment Reversal**

MacDonald et al. (2016) established the DiD framework for Operation Impact using staggered treatment *onset*. The extension naturally inverts this: treatment reversal occurs as impact zones are deactivated and SQF collapses differentially across precincts. The conditional FE Poisson framework (Model 1: `Y ~ treatment | year_pct_month`) can be extended by adding a `treatment_post` indicator for the post-enforcement era (2013–2016).

The project's existing staggered-DD extensions (Sun & Abraham, Callaway & Sant'Anna) provide heterogeneity-robust estimators that address concerns about forbidden comparisons in staggered designs. The Bacon (2021) decomposition in the replication confirms that 65% of the TWFE weight falls on clean treated-vs-untreated comparisons.

**2. Interrupted Time Series with Synthetic Controls**

Devi & Fryer (2020) demonstrate the synthetic control method for isolating the effects of policing policy shocks. For the NYC extension, former impact zones could be compared to synthetic counterfactuals constructed from never-treated blocks with similar pre-2013 crime trajectories. This approach is particularly powerful because it provides a visual counterfactual and is transparent about the quality of the pre-treatment match.

Tregle et al. (2025a) validate ITSA for hot spots policing, comparing a treatment city to control cities over seven years. An analogous within-NYC design could compare former impact zone blocks to structurally similar non-impact blocks using the ITS framework.

**3. Event Study Around Floyd v. NYC (August 2013)**

Cho et al. (2021) use event study designs around discrete policing shocks (officer deaths), estimating dynamic treatment effects across multiple pre- and post-shock periods. The extension plan's proposed ±24-month event study window around the Floyd ruling (August 12, 2013) follows this template. MacDonald et al.'s Model 3 already implements lead/lag indicators; the extension would apply the same structure to the enforcement withdrawal.

Devi & Fryer's finding that the *context* of enforcement withdrawal matters (viral vs. non-viral) suggests that the Floyd ruling — a judicial intervention without a viral deadly force incident — may produce a qualitatively different trajectory than the cities in their study. The event study design can test for this.

**4. Dose-Response with Continuous Treatment Intensity**

MacDonald et al.'s Model 4 decomposes treatment into probable-cause stops and non-probable-cause stops. The extension should model enforcement intensity as a continuous variable (stop counts, arrest rates) rather than a binary treatment indicator, since the post-2012 decline was not an on/off switch but a gradual (then precipitous) reduction in SQF activity. The Weisburd et al. (2015) space-time interaction framework provides a template for modeling continuous enforcement variation at high spatial and temporal resolution.

**5. Permutation Inference**

MacDonald et al.'s (2016) permutation test provides nonparametric validation that results are not driven by treatment timing artifacts. The same approach should be applied to the extension period, randomly reshuffling the timing of SQF decline across precincts to test whether the observed crime changes could arise by chance.

**Recommendation for the extension:** A multi-method approach combining (1) the existing conditional FE Poisson DiD framework with a post-enforcement indicator, (2) an event study around Floyd with ±24-month windows, and (3) dose-response models with continuous SQF intensity. These three approaches triangulate from different assumptions and address the key threats to identification (confounding events, gradual vs. abrupt treatment reversal, heterogeneous effects across zones and crime types).

## Phase 4: Finding the Blind Spots (The Post-SQF Era)

### Question 7: Consistently Identified Gaps in Limitations and Future Research Sections

Seven recurring gaps emerge across the corpus:

**Gap 1: Inability to separate police presence from police enforcement activity.**
This is the most frequently cited limitation. Weisburd et al. (2015) acknowledge they "cannot fully separate the effect of SQF from the effect of increased police presence." MacDonald et al. (2016) attempt to address this with the dose-response model (Model 4) but note that deployment and stop activity are mechanically correlated. The National Academies (2018) review identifies this as a field-wide limitation. Tregle et al. (2025b) provide the closest test by comparing HV (presence-only) to OF (presence + enforcement) strategies.

**Gap 2: Long-term sustainability and post-treatment effects.**
Sorg et al. (2013) find no residual deterrence after foot patrols withdraw, but their experiment lasted only 12–22 weeks. Weisburd & Telep (2014) identify the long-term sustainability of hot spots policing effects as a key unknown. No study in the corpus examines what happens to crime in areas that were treated for *nearly a decade* (as Operation Impact zones were from 2003–2012). The extension analysis directly fills this gap.

**Gap 3: The period of rapid SQF decline (2012–2016) is essentially unexamined.**
Sullivan & O'Keeffe (2017) examine a narrow window (the 2014–2015 slowdown), and Chalfin et al. (2024) show their results are not robust. No study in the corpus rigorously examines the multi-year structural decline in SQF from its peak of 685,724 stops in 2011 to under 50,000 by 2015 at the micro-geographic level. The Devi & Fryer (2020) framework is closest but examines DOJ investigations nationally, not the NYC-specific SQF collapse.

**Gap 4: Heterogeneous effects across crime types and neighborhoods.**
MacDonald et al. (2016) estimate effects for 10 crime categories but do not fully explore heterogeneity across neighborhood characteristics (poverty, racial composition, housing type). Petersen et al. (2023) note that the individual-level harms of stops are more pronounced for youth but do not examine neighborhood-level moderators. The NAS (2018) report calls for more research on whether proactive policing effects vary across community types.

**Gap 5: Measurement of community-level outcomes beyond crime counts.**
Tyler et al. (2014) measure legitimacy and cooperation, but only at a single point in time via survey. The corpus lacks longitudinal measures of how community attitudes, collective efficacy, and informal social control evolve before, during, and after intensive enforcement. Meares (2015) argues that the constitutional framework itself fails to capture these cumulative programmatic effects.

**Gap 6: Selection into treatment and endogeneity of impact zone designation.**
MacDonald et al. (2016) use fixed effects to control for time-invariant unobservables, but impact zones were selected on the basis of crime levels — creating potential regression-to-the-mean bias. The staggered activation of zones across years helps, but no study in the corpus employs an instrumental variable or regression discontinuity design that exploits plausibly exogenous variation in impact zone assignment.

**Gap 7: Jurisdictional vs. localized effects.**
Weisburd & Telep (2014) identify this as a key unknown. Tregle et al. (2025a) begin to address it with a single Texas case, but no study examines whether NYC's overall crime decline was partly attributable to the cumulative effect of Operation Impact across many precincts. The extension analysis could contribute here by examining citywide crime trends conditional on the aggregate intensity of impact zone coverage.

### Question 8: Under-Researched Variables That Could Explain Crime Remaining Low After SQF Plummets

The literature points to several alternative explanations and under-examined mechanisms:

**1. Community and neighborhood social dynamics.**
Tyler et al. (2014) document that SQF undermines police legitimacy, which in turn reduces willingness to cooperate with law enforcement. The logical corollary — that *removing* SQF could *restore* legitimacy and increase informal social control — is essentially untested. If the withdrawal of aggressive enforcement improved police-community relations, the resulting increase in witness cooperation, 911 calls, and informal guardianship could partially offset the loss of deterrence.

Fagan & MacDonald (2012) frame policing as an "urban amenity" — but an amenity that can become a burden when it takes the form of aggressive enforcement. The post-SQF period could test whether communities experienced the withdrawal as relief (restoring social capital) or as abandonment (reducing guardianship).

**2. Economic and demographic shifts.**
Chalfin & McCrary (2018) note that policing effects must be assessed against background economic conditions. NYC experienced significant gentrification, population change, and economic growth during the 2012–2016 period that could independently suppress crime. No study in the corpus explicitly controls for contemporaneous neighborhood-level economic changes when estimating policing effects.

**3. Incapacitation effects from prior enforcement.**
The massive arrest increases during Operation Impact (53% per MacDonald et al.) may have generated lasting incapacitation effects — high-risk offenders imprisoned during the Impact era remained incarcerated after SQF declined. This "capital stock" of incapacitation could sustain crime reductions independently of ongoing enforcement. No study in the corpus examines this mechanism.

**4. Target hardening and environmental design changes.**
Weisburd & Telep (2014) note that hot spots may be amenable to environmental design interventions that persist after police withdraw. If impact zone neighborhoods experienced physical improvements (lighting, surveillance cameras, building rehabilitation) during the 2003–2012 period, these structural changes could sustain crime reductions. This is completely unexamined in the corpus.

**5. Substitution of enforcement strategies.**
The post-SQF NYPD did not simply stop policing — it may have substituted other strategies (precision policing, focused deterrence, domain awareness systems, neighborhood coordination officers). Cho et al. (2021) show that marginal enforcement reductions do not increase crime, but they do not examine whether substitute strategies were deployed. The extension should account for whether new NYPD strategies replaced SQF as a crime control mechanism.

**6. Diffusion and technology effects.**
The 2012–2016 period saw widespread adoption of surveillance technology (CCTV, license plate readers, predictive policing algorithms) that could partially substitute for the deterrent function of police presence. No study in the corpus examines these technological substitutes.

**7. Reporting behavior changes.**
Sullivan & O'Keeffe (2017) use reported crime data, which may be affected by changes in reporting behavior. If aggressive enforcement deterred crime *reporting* (e.g., residents avoided police contact), then the measured crime reduction during Impact was partly artificial, and the post-SQF "increase" could simply reflect normalized reporting. Chalfin et al. (2024) raise this concern in their replication critique.

## Phase 5: The "Missing Corpus"

### Question 9: Most Critical External References Not in the Uploaded Papers

Based on bibliographic analysis across all 25 papers, the following 10 works are most frequently cited and foundational for the police surges / SQF / enforcement withdrawal literature, but are not included in the project's source corpus:

**1. Sherman & Weisburd (1995) — "General Deterrent Effects of Police Patrol in Crime 'Hot Spots': A Randomized Controlled Experiment"** (*Justice Quarterly*)
The foundational hot spots policing RCT. Cited by virtually every paper in the corpus. The Minneapolis Hot Spots Experiment demonstrated that directed patrol at 55 crime hot spots reduced crime and disorder. This is the empirical bedrock upon which the entire hot spots policing literature — and Operation Impact — is built.

**2. Weisburd, Bushway, Lum & Yang (2004) — "Trajectories of Crime at Places: A Longitudinal Study of Street Segments in the City of Seattle"** (*Criminology*)
Established the "law of crime concentration" — that crime clusters at a small number of micro-places and these concentrations are remarkably stable over time. Foundational for understanding why impact zones were designated where they were and whether crime concentrations persist after enforcement withdrawal.

**3. Cohen & Felson (1979) — "Social Change and Crime Rate Trends: A Routine Activity Approach"** (*American Sociological Review*)
The theoretical foundation of routine activities theory, which explains crime concentration through the convergence of motivated offenders, suitable targets, and absent guardians. Cited by Nagin et al. (2015) and the NAS (2018) report as the theoretical basis for why police presence affects crime.

**4. Zimring (2012) — *The City That Became Safe: New York's Lessons for Urban Crime and Its Control*** (Oxford University Press)
The most comprehensive analysis of NYC's historic crime decline. Cited by White (2014), Fagan & MacDonald (2012), and multiple other papers. Essential for understanding the macro context in which Operation Impact operated and for distinguishing Impact's effects from broader city trends.

**5. Fagan & Davies (2000) — "Street Stops and Broken Windows: Terry, Race, and Disorder in New York City"** (*Fordham Urban Law Journal*)
Early empirical analysis of NYPD SQF linking aggressive enforcement to racial disparities. Cited by Gelman et al. (2007), Tyler et al. (2014), and Meares (2015). Provides the pre-Impact baseline for understanding SQF's evolution from investigative tool to mass enforcement program.

**6. Terry v. Ohio, 392 U.S. 1 (1968)**
The Supreme Court decision authorizing investigative stops based on reasonable suspicion. Cited by every SQF paper in the corpus. Meares (2015) argues that Terry's individual-incident framework fundamentally mismatches the programmatic deployment of SQF in Operation Impact.

**7. Floyd v. City of New York, 959 F. Supp. 2d 540 (S.D.N.Y. 2013)**
The federal court ruling that found NYPD's SQF program unconstitutional as implemented. This is the key structural break for the extension analysis. Cited by multiple papers but not analyzed in depth in the corpus.

**8. Wilson & Kelling (1982) — "Broken Windows: The Police and Neighborhood Safety"** (*The Atlantic*)
The theoretical foundation of broken windows policing, which motivated NYPD's aggressive enforcement of low-level offenses. Cited as background by nearly every paper in the corpus. Sullivan & O'Keeffe (2017) frame their findings as a challenge to this theory.

**9. Becker (1968) — "Crime and Punishment: An Economic Approach"** (*Journal of Political Economy*)
The foundational rational choice / deterrence model in economics. Cited by Chalfin & McCrary (2018) and Nagin et al. (2015). Provides the theoretical basis for the view that increasing the perceived probability of apprehension (through police presence and SQF) deters crime.

**10. Sampson, Raudenbush & Earls (1997) — "Neighborhoods and Violent Crime: A Multilevel Study of Collective Efficacy"** (*Science*)
Established collective efficacy as the key community-level mechanism for crime control — residents' shared willingness to intervene for the common good. Cited by Tyler et al. (2014) and the NAS (2018) report. Critical for understanding whether aggressive enforcement (SQF) undermines or enhances the community-level social processes that independently suppress crime.
